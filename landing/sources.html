🧠 Neuroscience

The biological basis for belief change. These researchers established that the brain physically rewires itself in response to experience — the mechanism that makes belief reprogramming possible.

Lisa Feldman Barrett
Constructed emotion theory. Emotions are not hardwired — the brain constructs them from past experience, interoception, and context. This means emotional patterns can be reconstructed.
How Emotions Are Made (2017)
Provides the neuroscientific justification for belief reprogramming: if the brain constructs emotional experience, changing beliefs changes the construction.
Barrett, L. F. (2017). How emotions are made: The secret life of the brain. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.
Bruce Lipton
Stem cell biologist. Demonstrated that cellular behavior is controlled by environment (epigenetics), not DNA. Beliefs shape the biochemical environment that determines gene expression.
The Biology of Belief (2005)
The biology behind belief's physical impact. Used in our engine to contextualize why believing differently changes your biochemistry.
Lipton, B. H. (2005). The biology of belief: Unleashing the power of consciousness, matter & miracles. Mountain of Love Productions.
Candace Pert (1946–2013)
Neuroscientist and pharmacologist. Discovered the opiate receptor. Demonstrated that neuropeptides carry emotional information throughout the body — the biochemical basis of "emotions stored in the body."
Molecules of Emotion (1997)
Provides the biochemical bridge between Bradley Nelson's trapped emotions and Lisa Feldman Barrett's constructed emotion theory.
Pert, C. B. (1997). Molecules of emotion: Why you feel the way you feel. Scribner.
Joe Dispenza
Chiropractor and neuroscience researcher. Popularized the neuroscience of belief change through meditation. Demonstrated that mental rehearsal produces the same brain changes as physical practice.
Breaking the Habit of Being Yourself (2012)
The daily practice model — consistency over intensity — that our workbook's 30-day structure emulates.
Dispenza, J. (2012). Breaking the habit of being yourself: How to lose your mind and create a new one. Hay House.
Norman Doidge
Psychiatrist and neuroplasticity researcher. Documented the brain's lifelong capacity to reorganize itself — neuroplasticity is not limited to childhood.
The Brain That Changes Itself (2007)
Provides the core evidence that belief change at any age is biologically possible — the brain is never "done."
Doidge, N. (2007). The brain that changes itself: Stories of personal triumph from the frontiers of brain science. Penguin.

🧩 Psychology & Cognitive Science

The clinical tradition of belief restructuring. These pioneers established that thoughts and beliefs can be systematically identified, challenged, and replaced — the therapeutic foundation of everything we do.

Aaron Beck (1921–2021)
Father of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). Developed the cognitive model: thoughts → emotions → behaviors. Identified cognitive distortions (all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, etc.).
Cognitive Therapy and the Emotional Disorders (1976)
Our belief-identification logic (which beliefs are distorted? which are factual?) draws heavily from Beck's cognitive distortion framework.
Beck, A. T. (1976). Cognitive therapy and the emotional disorders. International Universities Press.
Albert Ellis (1913–2007)
Developed Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT). The ABC model (Activating event → Belief → Consequence). "People are not disturbed by things, but by the view they take of them."
A Guide to Rational Living (1961)
The ABC model underlies our belief structuring: identify the trigger, find the belief, anticipate the consequence of changing it.
Ellis, A., & Harper, R. A. (1961). A guide to rational living. Wilshire Book Company.
Martin Seligman
Founder of Positive Psychology. Discovered learned helplessness and its counterpart, learned optimism. Demonstrated that explanatory style (how you explain events to yourself) determines resilience.
Learned Optimism (1990)
Our replacement beliefs are designed to shift explanatory style — not from negative to toxic-positive, but from helpless to empowered.
Seligman, M. E. P. (1990). Learned optimism: How to change your mind and your life. Knopf.
Carol Dweck
Growth mindset vs. fixed mindset. Demonstrated that beliefs about the nature of ability (fixed or malleable) dramatically affect learning, resilience, and achievement.
Mindset: The New Psychology of Success (2006)
Many of our belief pairs directly address fixed-mindset statements ("I'm not the kind of person who...") and provide growth-mindset alternatives.
Dweck, C. S. (2006). Mindset: The new psychology of success. Random House.
Daniel Kahneman
Nobel laureate. Identified System 1 (fast, automatic, subconscious) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, conscious) thinking. Cognitive biases that distort belief formation.
Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011)
System 1 maps to the conditioned/subconscious beliefs we target. System 2 is the deliberate replacement process our workbooks guide.
Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, fast and slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.

🤲 Somatic & Body-Based Work

The body remembers what the mind forgets. These practitioners established that trauma and limiting beliefs are stored not just in the brain but in the body's tissues, nervous system, and movement patterns.

Bessel van der Kolk
Psychiatrist and trauma researcher. Established that trauma is stored in the body, not just the mind. "The body keeps the score."
The Body Keeps the Score (2014)
The scientific foundation for our insistence that belief work must be embodied — not just intellectual. Our engine connects beliefs to specific body locations via HD centers.
van der Kolk, B. A. (2014). The body keeps the score: Brain, mind, and body in the healing of trauma. Viking.
Peter Levine
Developer of Somatic Experiencing®. Observed that animals in the wild discharge trauma through physical trembling — humans override this with cognition.
Waking the Tiger (1997)
Provides the framework for our belief-release work — the "feeling it in the body" step before replacing a belief.
Levine, P. A. (1997). Waking the tiger: Healing trauma. North Atlantic Books.
Stephen Porges
Developed Polyvagal Theory. The autonomic nervous system has three states (social engagement, fight/flight, shutdown). Safety must precede belief change.
The Polyvagal Theory (2011)
Our workbook structure prioritizes safety cues and grounding before deep belief work — direct application of Polyvagal sequencing.
Porges, S. W. (2011). The polyvagal theory: Neurophysiological foundations of emotions, attachment, communication, and self-regulation. W. W. Norton.
